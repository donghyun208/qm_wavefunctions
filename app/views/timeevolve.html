Time Evolution


The equation that governs time evolution in quantum mechanics is the Time Dependent Schrodinger Equation: $$ i\hbar\frac{\partial}{\partial t} \Psi(r,t) = \hat{H}\Psi(r,t) $$ For time-independent Hamiltonians, the general solution is given as: $$ \begin{align} i\hbar\frac{\partial}{\partial t} \Psi(r,t) &= \hat{H}\Psi(r,t)\\ \frac{\partial}{\partial t} \Psi(r,t) &= -\frac{i}{\hbar}\hat{H}\Psi(r,t) \end{align} $$ $$ \Psi(r,t) = e^{-\frac{i}{\hbar}\hat{H}t}\Psi(r,0)\\ $$ where $\Psi(r,0)$ is the initial condition. This means if we know $\Psi(r,0)$, the state of our system at time $t=0$, and we know the Hamiltonian, we can know what the state of the system at any future time as well. The exponential operator $e^{-\frac{i}{\hbar}\hat{H}t}$ is called the time evolution operator, and satisfies: $$ e^{-\frac{i}{\hbar}\hat{H}t} \psi_n(x,0) = e^{-\frac{i}{\hbar}E_nt} \psi_n(x,0) $$ for the energy eigenstates $\psi_n$. (This can be shown to be true if you Taylor expand the time evolution operator, and apply the Time Independent Schrodinger Equation).
Now, we can write down the expression for an arbitrary time-dependent wavefunction consisting of the first three eigenstates:
$$ \Psi(x,t) = c_1e^{\frac{i}{\hbar} E_1 t} \psi_1(x) + c_2e^{\frac{i}{\hbar} E_2 t} \psi_2(x) + c_3e^{\frac{i}{\hbar} E_3 t} \psi_3(x) $$
Note that all of the time dependence is in the phase of the coefficients in front of each eigenstate. This means that time dependence of a wavefunction enters into our expression by changing the phase of each eigenstate, at a frequency proportional to the energy of that eigenstate.
